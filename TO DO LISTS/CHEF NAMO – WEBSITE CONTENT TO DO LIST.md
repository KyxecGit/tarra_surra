My name is Chef Namo. I was born in Bangkok and raised in Sakaeo a small city in eastern Thailand, where I lived until I was twelve years old. Some of my earliest memories are of standing beside my grandmother and my father in the kitchen, helping prepare meals for our family. What began as playful curiosity slowly became a meaningful daily ritual. Cooking was how I learned to express care, gratitude, and connection. Alongside this, I was deeply involved in academia, student government, and Thai cultural arts, always seeking opportunities to learn and contribute.

Through my dedication to Thai cultural arts, I had the honor of traveling to China, France, Mongolia, and Hungary to help share Thailand’s artistic heritage with international audiences. These experiences shaped me profoundly. Representing my country abroad taught me humility and pride at the same time. It was during these journeys that I developed a deep love for sharing Thailand — not only its art and traditions, but also its flavors and hospitality — with the world.

My formal culinary training began at Dusit Thani Culinary School in Bangkok, where I completed my Bachelor of Arts. There, I refined the skills that were first nurtured at home. My philosophy of Thai cuisine is rooted in three essential qualities: flavor, variety, and shareability. Thai food is meant to be experienced together. At its heart is balance — the thoughtful harmony of acidity, sweetness, saltiness, bitterness, and fattiness. Each element plays a role, guiding the palate through contrast and cohesion. For me, Thai cuisine is both precise and generous, complex yet welcoming.

Tarra represents my commitment to honoring these values. It is a reflection of my upbringing, my education, and my experiences sharing Thailand with others around the world. Through Tarra, I hope to create a space where tradition is respected, flavors are balanced with intention, and every meal is an opportunity to gather, connect, and celebrate Thai culture with sincerity and warmth.

<br/>Chef Namo was born in Bangkok and raised in Sakaeo, a quiet province in eastern Thailand near the border. Growing up in the countryside allowed him to live closely connected to nature. His days were surrounded by open land, fruit trees, and small gardens near his home. Many ingredients came not only from local markets, but directly from the land around him — herbs picked fresh, fruits harvested in season, and vegetables grown nearby. Life in Sakaeo moved at a slower, gentler pace, leaving more time to gather as a family and truly enjoy meals together. His father cooked almost every day, and nearly every meal was prepared at home.

Because Sakaeo is a border city, the community reflected a blend of cultures and traditions. This early exposure to diverse people and ingredients gave Chef Namo an appreciation for the rich and flavorful diversity of Southeast Asian cuisine. He tasted bold herbs, fermented flavors, grilled meats, and vibrant salads long before he understood them technically. When he later moved to Bangkok for high school, his world expanded even further. In the international energy of the capital, he discovered Japanese and Italian cuisines, along with regional Thai dishes he had not encountered growing up. The contrast between the quiet countryside and the dynamic city shaped both his palate and his curiosity.

From a young age, Chef Namo was deeply influenced by his grandmother and his father. Cooking was an act of love in his family. His grandmother taught him patience — how to prepare curry pastes by hand, how to balance flavors instinctively, and how to respect ingredients. His father demonstrated discipline and consistency, showing him that daily cooking is a responsibility and a privilege. These early lessons instilled in him a belief that food is not only nourishment, but a reflection of care and cultural identity.

Chef Namo later pursued formal training at Le Cordon Bleu Culinary School at Dusit Thani College in Bangkok, where he studied both Thai and French cuisine. There, he learned classical techniques, kitchen structure, and refinement of flavor. His education strengthened his foundation, allowing him to bridge traditional Thai principles with professional culinary discipline.

The idea for Tarra was born during the pandemic. After spending nearly a decade cooking primarily Western cuisine, he finally found the time to cook Thai food at home again. In doing so, he rediscovered his deep love for the flavors of his childhood. As he prepared meals for friends and family, their encouragement sparked something in him. He realized how difficult it was to find truly authentic, non-Westernized Thai flavors in restaurants abroad. The thought stayed with him — he felt both excitement and responsibility to share genuine Thai cuisine with Chicago. Tarra became his answer to that calling: a space dedicated to honest flavors and cultural integrity.

Over the years, Chef Namo has served in several executive chef roles, including at The Franklin Room, Madison Bar and Kitchen, and Union Sushi. These leadership positions strengthened his ability to manage teams, refine menus, and create thoughtful dining experiences. Yet no matter the concept, he carried with him the foundational lessons of balance, generosity, and respect for ingredients.

Today, what drives Chef Namo is a desire to represent Thailand with sincerity. He is motivated by the opportunity to share real Thai flavors, to preserve culinary traditions, and to create spaces where food brings people together. Through his work, he hopes to honor his family, his upbringing, and the culture that shaped him — offering each guest not just a meal, but a meaningful connection to Thailand.

Tone: Narrative, inspirational, prestigious.

Length: Full biography page.
